Potato Early Blight

Introduction

Potato early blight is a common fungal disease that can cause significant yield losses in potato crops. It is caused by the fungus Alternaria solani, which can also infect tomatoes and other members of the Solanaceae family. Early blight can occur anywhere potatoes are grown, but it is most common in warm, humid climates.

Symptoms

The first symptoms of early blight typically appear on the lower, older leaves of potato plants. Small, brown or black spots develop on the leaves, often surrounded by a yellow halo. As the disease progresses, the spots enlarge and coalesce, causing the leaves to turn yellow and drop.

Early blight can also infect the stems and tubers of potato plants. Stem lesions are typically dark brown or black and elongated in shape. Tuber lesions are sunken and brown or black in color.

Causes

The fungus Alternaria solani survives in infected plant debris and soil. It can also be spread by wind, rain, and irrigation water. Spores of the fungus germinate and infect potato plants when the leaves are wet.

Early blight is most likely to occur during warm, humid weather. Frequent rainfall and overhead irrigation can increase the risk of infection.

Control

Plant resistant varieties. There are a number of potato varieties that are resistant to early blight.

Use crop rotation. Planting potatoes in a different location each year can help to reduce the amount of the fungus in the soil.

Avoid overhead irrigation. Overhead irrigation can keep the leaves wet for extended periods of time, which favors the development of early blight.

Apply fungicides. Fungicides can be applied to potato plants to protect them from early blight infection. However, it is important to follow the label directions carefully and to rotate fungicides to prevent the fungus from developing resistance.
